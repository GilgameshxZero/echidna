Sterling’s approximation intutions

We first derive a suboptimal approximation for n!n!n!, which bears some similarity to the derivation of the bound for a harmonic and prime harmonic series:

f(n)=n!  ⟹  ln⁡f(n)=ln⁡1+ln⁡2+⋯+ln⁡n≈∫1n(ln⁡x)dx=[xln⁡x−x]1n(by parts)=nln⁡n−n+1  ⟹  f(n)≈(eln⁡n)n/en/e≈(n/e)n.\begin{aligned} f(n)&=n!\\ \implies \ln f(n)&=\ln 1+\ln 2+\cdots+\ln n\\ &\approx \int_1^n (\ln x)dx\\ &=\Big[x\ln x-x\Big]_1^n &\text{(by parts)}\\ &=n\ln n-n+1\\ \implies f(n)&\approx (e^{\ln n})^n/e^n/e\\ &\approx (n/e)^n. \end{aligned}f(n)⟹lnf(n)⟹f(n)​=n!=ln1+ln2+⋯+lnn≈∫1n​(lnx)dx=[xlnx−x]1n​=nlnn−n+1≈(elnn)n/en/e≈(n/e)n.​(by parts)​

Sterling’s approximation includes an additional (asymptotic) factor:

f(n)=n!≈2πn(n/e)nf(n)=n!\approx \sqrt{2\pi n}(n/e)^n f(n)=n!≈2πn​(n/e)n

which is most easily derived using the Gamma function, and transforming parts of the integral into two dimensions.

Indeed, one will notice that the 2πn\sqrt{2\pi n}2πn​ factor bears similarity to the constant seen in the Normal distribution, which also uses a similar integral transformation technique. This similarity is likely the most intuitive way to remember the constant factor for Sterling’s.
