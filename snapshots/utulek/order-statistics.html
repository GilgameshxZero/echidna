For the iii-th order statistic to be at most xxx, at least iii of the samples must be at most xxx. Each of these are disjoint events, since they have a different count of samples which are at most xxx and at least xxx. Hence, we sum these disjoint events up to get the final CDF.

This PDF actually has a highly intuitive interpretation, which is as follows. First, we choose i−1i-1i−1 positions to be smaller than xxx. Of the remaining n−i+1n-i+1n−i+1 positions, we choose 111 to be equal to xxx. This is (ni−1)(n−i+1)=n(n−1i−1){n\choose i-1}(n-i+1)=n{n-1\choose i-1}(i−1n​)(n−i+1)=n(i−1n−1​) choices for positioning.

It remains to assign numbers to each of the positions. There are i−1i-1i−1 numbers less than xxx to assign, one number equal to xxx, and n−in-in−i numbers greater than xxx to assign. This gives the three fff and FFF terms in the PDF.

Order statistics under uniform population

Readers may recognize the form of the PDF also as close to the familiar Beta distribution:

fi(x)=n!(i−1)!(n−i)!f(x)(F(x))i−1(1−F(x))n−i∝Beta(i,n−i+1)\begin{aligned} f_i(x)&=\frac{n!}{(i-1)!(n-i)!}f(x)(F(x))^{i-1}(1-F(x))^{n-i}\\ &\propto Beta(i,n-i+1) \end{aligned}fi​(x)​=(i−1)!(n−i)!n!​f(x)(F(x))i−1(1−F(x))n−i∝Beta(i,n−i+1)​

Each order statistic, when interpreted as a RV itself, follows a Beta distribution, as long as f(x)f(x)f(x) is uniform (i.e. population XXX follows a uniform distribution).

Furthermore, there is a clever simplification of the distribution of order statistics differences. That is, for j>ij>ij>i, the distribution of X(j)−X(i)X_{(j)}-X_{(i)}X(j)​−X(i)​ is necessarily the same as the distribution of X(j−i)X_{(j-i)}X(j−i)​ should the underlying distribution f(x)f(x)f(x) be uniform.

Without directly convoluting the PDFs, we may consider instead n+1n+1n+1 samples drawn uniformly upon a circle. By splitting the circle at the first sample, we achieve the same distribution of points as we would if we had drawn nnn points uniformly from a line. Hence, the differences of order statistics are shift-invariant.

More generally, we have

Beta(j,n−j+1)−Beta(i,n−i+1)=Beta(j−i,n−(i−j)+1).Beta(j,n-j+1)-Beta(i,n-i+1)=Beta(j-i,n-(i-j)+1). Beta(j,n−j+1)−Beta(i,n−i+1)=Beta(j−i,n−(i−j)+1).

Of course, the shift-invariance of differences also applies when adding compatible Beta distributions as well.

Discrete RVs

Importantly, this PDF is for continuous variables, and the PDF (PMF, more accurately, but we will continue to refer to it as the PDF) for discrete RVs is slightly different. To compute it, we again use the same reasoning to compute the CDF, which should be identical, before subtracting two neighboring CDFs to compute the PDF of a single point xxx.

P(X(i)≤x)=∑k=in(nk)(P(X≤x))k(1−P(X≤x))n−kP(X(i)<x)=∑k=in(nk)(P(X<x))k(1−P(X<x))n−k))  ⟹  P(X(i)=x)=P(X(i)≤x)−P(X(i)<x)\begin{aligned} P(X_{(i)}\leq x)&=\sum_{k=i}^n{n\choose k}(P(X\leq x))^k(1-P(X\leq x))^{n-k}\\ P(X_{(i)}< x)&=\sum_{k=i}^n{n\choose k}(P(X<x))^k(1-P(X<x))^{n-k}))\\ \implies P(X_{(i)}=x)&=P(X_{(i)}\leq x)-P(X_{(i)}< x) \end{aligned}P(X(i)​≤x)P(X(i)​<x)⟹P(X(i)​=x)​=k=i∑n​(kn​)(P(X≤x))k(1−P(X≤x))n−k=k=i∑n​(kn​)(P(X<x))k(1−P(X<x))n−k))=P(X(i)​≤x)−P(X(i)​<x)​

which as you can imagine can be somewhat tedious to compute. Instead, look for applications of symmetry, or complementary computations.

Of particular note to problem-solving for order statistics is the equality for non-negative RVs

E[X]=∫0∞P(X≥x)dx\begin{aligned} E[X]&=\int_0^\infty P(X\geq x)dx \end{aligned}E[X]​=∫0∞​P(X≥x)dx​

The intuitive explanation is that the PDF for larger xxx are added to the answer more than the PDF for smaller xxx. In fact, the PDF for some xxx is added exactly xxx times in the expectation, as desired. The discrete version of this is

E[X]=∑1∞P(X≥x)dxE[X]=\sum_1^\infty P(X\geq x)dx E[X]=1∑∞​P(X≥x)dx

with the bottom limit changing to 111 as intuition would have it.
